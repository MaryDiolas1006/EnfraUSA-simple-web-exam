Hepatitis B and C Virus Coinfection With HIV

This review includes a Sunday symposium covering hepatitis C virus (HCV) epidemiology,[1] molecular virology,[2] immunopathogenesis,[3] and treatment, as well as a Monday poster session covering HIV coinfection with hepatitis B virus (HBV) and HCV.

HVC Natural History

Following virus inoculation, HCV RNA can be detected within days to weeks in blood and liver, usually associated with marked elevations of ALT. Antibody appears in 8-10 weeks, so there is a window period during which primary infection can be demonstrated, similar to HIV. In most cases, a chronic persistent infection ensues, which only occasionally results in cirrhosis or hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC). Viral clearance is seen in about 15% of infected individuals, and is still associated with HCV-specific antibody and T cells, but there is no progression to cirrhosis. This is in stark contrast to HIV infection, where clearance after primary infection with detectable RNA is probably extremely rare. In those with chronic HCV infection, stable persistent infection without clinical disease is seen in the majority of individuals. Progression to cirrhosis and end-stage liver disease occurs in fewer than 20%.

Treatment of HCV Improving, Good Responses Even in HIV Coinfection

In the final talk of the Sunday symposium, Mark Sulkowski gave an overview of HCV treatment, and there were several recent studies of treatment of HCV in HIV-coinfected patients presented in posters on Monday. Recommendations for management of HIV/HCV infection were presented:

  • All HIV-infected individuals should be screened for HCV antibody

  • Alcohol use should be limited

  • Screen for HAV antibody; if negative, give HAV vaccine

  • Monitor liver enzymes, especially with HAART

  • Patients should be evaluated for extent of liver disease and the need for treatment

For more information: JAMA 2000
